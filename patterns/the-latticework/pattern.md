# The Latticework

## Protocol  ← TLDR zone (always at the top)

**Trigger:** Any high-stakes decision where you're relying on a single discipline's framework to reach a conclusion.

**Steps:**
1. Name the discipline you're currently reasoning from (e.g., "I'm doing finance here").
2. List 3-5 other disciplines that touch this problem (psychology, biology, physics, history, engineering, etc.).
3. For each discipline, pull the 1-2 most relevant models and apply them to the decision independently.
4. Look for convergence: if 4 of 5 discipline-lenses reach the same conclusion, act with confidence. If they diverge, the divergence is the signal — resolve it before deciding.
5. Ask: "What model am I missing that could invert this conclusion?" Run that model explicitly.

**Anti-pattern:** The man with a hammer — using the one discipline you know best to analyze everything, mistaking fluency for correctness.

**Hard rule:** Never commit to a major position that only one discipline's models support.

## The Book  ← depth zone (always at the bottom)

### The Pattern

Munger's defining move was to build — deliberately, over decades — a toolkit of ~100 mental models drawn from the full range of human disciplines, then run every important judgment through that toolkit as a convergence checklist. The insight is not "know many things" but rather: **every major error in judgment is caused by a missing model**. The man using only finance misses the psychology. The man using only psychology misses the physics. Convergence across disciplines is how you catch what any single lens would miss.

### Protocol (extended)

**Step 1 — Name your current lens.**
Before reasoning, identify which discipline is doing the work. Most people skip this and never realize they're in a hammer-nail trap.

**Step 2 — Enumerate adjacent disciplines.**
Ask: which other fields study this same underlying reality? A business decision involves psychology (buyer behavior), biology (competition and survival), physics (throughput and constraints), mathematics (compounding and probability), and history (analogous situations). Name them.

**Step 3 — Apply each lens independently.**
Don't blend them yet. Run finance, then psychology, then biology as separate analyses. Each discipline has its own vocabulary and failure modes — blending them early lets your dominant lens corrupt the others.

**Step 4 — Look for convergence, flag divergence.**
If the lenses agree, the conclusion is robust. If they diverge, you have found the real problem — not the surface question but the underlying tension between frameworks. Resolve that tension explicitly.

**Step 5 — Explicitly ask for the inverting model.**
Before deciding, name the model that would make you wrong. Run it. If it has no force, proceed. If it has force, you've found the real risk.

**Step 6 — Build the toolkit over time.**
This pattern only works if you've done the pre-work: reading across disciplines, not just deep in one. Munger's checklist was decades in the making. The latticework requires investment before the decision moment.

### Anti-Pattern (extended)

**The specialist trap.** A trained economist sees incentives everywhere. A trained engineer sees systems everywhere. A trained psychologist sees cognitive bias everywhere. Each is correct in their domain and wrong by extrapolation. The specialist's failure is invisible to them — they don't know what they don't see.

**The superficial multi-disciplinarian.** Knowing the names of mental models is not the same as being able to apply them under pressure. Munger specifically distinguished between having a list of models and actually holding them as executable tools. The anti-pattern fires when someone collects frameworks as intellectual decoration rather than operational instruments.

**The false convergence.** Using multiple frameworks that share the same underlying assumption (e.g., three financial models that all assume efficient markets) creates false confidence. True convergence requires genuinely independent lenses with different ontologies.

### Examples

**See's Candies (1972):** Benjamin Graham's framework — the one Buffett had been trained on — would not justify paying 3x book value for See's. It was a "cigar butt" stock at best. Munger applied consumer psychology (what does a box of See's Candies mean to a California family on Valentine's Day?), competitive moat theory (why hasn't any competitor been able to replicate this?), and pricing power analysis (what happens to earnings if we raise prices 10%?). The convergence of three lenses that Graham's single framework missed made the case airtight. See's became one of Berkshire's most important investments — both financially and as proof of concept for the latticework.

**The 1994 USC Speech:** Munger walked through a pharmaceutical distribution company analysis live, pulling models from thermodynamics (entropy as organizational decay), evolutionary biology (competitive selection), psychology (incentive-caused bias in sales forces), and accounting (distinguishing reported from economic earnings). The exercise demonstrated that each lens added irreplaceable signal. Published versions of this speech circulated for decades as a practical teaching document.

### Practitioners

**Charlie Munger (1924–2023):** Vice Chairman of Berkshire Hathaway, partner to Warren Buffett for over 50 years. Munger's explicit intellectual project was to read across every major discipline — physics, biology, psychology, economics, mathematics, engineering, history — and build an executable model from each. He described his method in speeches, interviews, and the book *Poor Charlie's Almanack*. His core claim: the biggest money and judgment mistakes come from using a single model. The latticework is the systematic remedy.

**Benjamin Franklin (1706–1790):** Franklin's polymath practice was structurally identical to the latticework, though he never named it as such. His invention of the lightning rod combined electrical theory (charge accumulation), meteorology (storm path prediction), structural engineering (conductor routing to ground), and empirical observation (field tests). No single discipline produced the solution; the convergence of all four did.

**Leonardo da Vinci (1452–1519):** Da Vinci's notebooks explicitly show cross-discipline pressure-testing: his observations on fluid dynamics informed his anatomical drawings (blood as hydraulic system), which informed his mechanical designs (joints as levers), which informed his flying machine specifications (bird wing loading transferred to fabric and wood). Each domain stress-tested conclusions reached in the others.

### Historical Events

**See's Candies Acquisition (1972):** Munger persuaded Buffett to pay $25 million — roughly 3x book value — for a California candy company. Graham's framework said no. Munger's latticework (consumer psychology + moat theory + pricing power) said yes. By 2023, See's had generated over $2 billion in cumulative pre-tax earnings for Berkshire. The acquisition is Exhibit A for what the latticework catches that single-framework analysis misses.

**LTCM Collapse (1998):** Long-Term Capital Management employed two Nobel laureates and the most sophisticated stochastic finance models available. Their framework was internally consistent and mathematically rigorous — within its discipline. It had no model for political contagion (Russia default), no model for liquidity spirals (all players rushing exits simultaneously), and no model for correlated behavior under stress. The latticework would have flagged all three. The fund lost $4.6 billion in six weeks.

**Berkshire's Technology Bubble Avoidance (1999–2000):** Munger and Buffett were publicly mocked for missing the internet boom. Their latticework — applying historical bubble psychology, intrinsic value mathematics, and competitive moat analysis simultaneously — flagged the divergence between market prices and any rational convergence of models. The S&P fell 49% from peak. Berkshire held.

### Lineage

The latticework's intellectual lineage runs through every tradition that insisted on cross-disciplinary rigor. Aristotle organized human knowledge into categories precisely to enable cross-domain reasoning — his *Nicomachean Ethics* applies concepts from biology (teleology), physics (final cause), and psychology (habituation). Francis Bacon's *Novum Organum* (1620) argued that natural philosophy required induction across many domains rather than deduction from a single authority. Benjamin Franklin practiced the latticework operationally in the 18th century. Munger named it, systematized it, and demonstrated it in investment decisions across six decades.

### Origin

The latticework as an explicit named framework originates with Munger's public speeches of the 1980s and 1990s, particularly the USC Business School address of 1994 and the Harvard Law School address of 1995. But the underlying practice — using convergent models from multiple disciplines before committing — is older than any single practitioner.

Aristotle's organizing insight was that different domains study the same underlying reality from different angles; the truth about any phenomenon is what survives when all legitimate angles have been applied. Munger read this through a decidedly practical lens: the same convergence that prevents philosophical error also prevents investment error. The latticework is what Aristotle's *Organon* looks like when applied to a decision rather than a proof.
