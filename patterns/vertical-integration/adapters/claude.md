# Vertical Integration — Carnegie Method

When a system depends on external suppliers or intermediaries for critical inputs and cost, quality, or reliability is suffering, apply the Carnegie method: own the chain, optimize every link, reinvest the savings.

## Protocol

1. **Map every link** in the value chain from raw input to end customer. For each link, note who controls it, what margin they extract, and what failure modes they create.
2. **Identify the bleeding link** — the one with the highest margin extraction, lowest reliability, or greatest competitive exposure.
3. **Acquire or build that link.** Don't diversify — integrate along the chain.
4. **Drive cost out ruthlessly.** Measure every sub-process in the acquired link. Ownership without optimization is just overhead.
5. **Reinvest savings into the next weakest link.** Each integration funds the next. The chain compounds.
6. **Stop when managing the next link costs more than the margin it leaks.** Not every link should be owned.

## Anti-Pattern

Owning links without optimizing them. Integration without cost discipline is the GM trap — captive divisions that cost more than open-market suppliers. Ownership is not the pattern. Ownership + relentless measurement + cost discipline is the pattern.

## Hard Rule

Never acquire a link you cannot measure and improve. If you can't see inside it, you can't drive cost out of it.

## Decision Check

Before recommending integration, verify:
- Is the dependency causing real cost, quality, or reliability problems? (Not hypothetical ones)
- Can you actually optimize the link better than the current supplier? (Do you have domain knowledge or scale advantage?)
- Will the management overhead stay below the margin you recapture?

If any answer is no, the move is a better contract, not acquisition.
