# Steve Jobs — Taste Gate

## Protocol  ← TLDR zone (always at the top)

**Trigger:** Options are multiplying — feature lists growing, design alternatives accumulating, scope expanding, or no single clear vision governing what stays and what goes

**Steps:**
1. DEFINE THE EXPERIENCE — state in one sentence what this should *feel like* to the person using it
2. KILL EVERYTHING THAT DOESN'T SERVE THAT SENTENCE — if a feature, option, or element doesn't directly make the experience sentence true, cut it. No "nice to have."
3. INTERROGATE WHAT REMAINS — for each surviving element, ask: "Is this the *best* version of this, or just an acceptable one?" Replace acceptable with best or cut it.
4. SAY IT BACK — describe the result as if pitching it to someone in 10 seconds. If the pitch isn't immediately compelling, repeat from Step 2.

**Anti-pattern:** Keeping options open — adding features "because users might want them" without a governing vision of what the experience must be

**Hard rule:** No element survives without a direct line to the experience sentence. "Useful" is not sufficient — it must be *essential to the feeling*.

## The Book  ← depth zone (always at the bottom)

### The Pattern

Steve Jobs made one move his entire career: he decided what something should *feel like*, and then killed everything that didn't serve that feeling. Not efficiency (that's Musk). Not simplicity for its own sake. *Taste* — an opinionated judgment about what deserves to exist, applied as a hard gate that most options cannot pass.

The result looks like minimalism, but it isn't. It's curation. Jobs didn't want fewer things — he wanted only the *right* things. The iPhone wasn't simple because simplicity is good. It was simple because Jobs had a specific vision of what holding a phone should feel like, and everything that violated that feeling was killed — including the keyboard that every phone maker assumed was mandatory.

"People think focus means saying yes to the thing you've got to focus on. But that's not what it means at all. It means saying no to the hundred other good ideas that there are."

### Protocol (extended)

**Step 1 — DEFINE THE EXPERIENCE**
- State in one sentence what the end result should *feel like* to the person using it
- This is not a feature spec. It's a feeling: "It should feel like the computer disappeared and you're just touching your music." (iPod) "It should feel like you're holding the internet in your hand." (iPhone)
- If you can't write the sentence, you don't have a product yet — stop and find the sentence before doing anything else

**Step 2 — KILL EVERYTHING THAT DOESN'T SERVE THAT SENTENCE**
- Every feature, every option, every design element gets tested against the experience sentence
- The test is binary: does this directly make the experience sentence true? Yes → stays. No → cut.
- "Nice to have" is the sound of taste failing. There is no "nice to have" — there's essential and there's cut.
- Target: most things get cut. If you haven't killed at least half, your taste filter isn't tight enough.

**Step 3 — INTERROGATE WHAT REMAINS**
- For each surviving element: "Is this the *best* version, or just an acceptable one?"
- Acceptable is the enemy. A rounded corner that's 90% right ships to millions of people being 10% wrong.
- If the best version doesn't exist yet, build it. If you can't build it, cut the element entirely — half-done is worse than absent.

**Step 4 — SAY IT BACK**
- Describe the result as a 10-second pitch. Not what it does — what it *is*.
- If the pitch isn't immediately, viscerally compelling, the curation isn't done. Return to Step 2.
- Jobs rehearsed keynotes for weeks. The pitch isn't vanity — it's the final quality gate. If you can't pitch it cleanly, the product isn't clean.

### Anti-Pattern (extended)

Keeping options open. Adding features "because users might want them." Offering three ways to do something instead of deciding which one is right. Designing by committee — asking what everyone wants instead of deciding what the thing should *be*.

The anti-pattern produces products that are capable but have no soul. They do everything adequately and nothing memorably. Every feature is defensible. None is essential. The experience sentence was never written, so nothing gets cut.

Specific violations:
- **"Let the user decide"** — offering preferences and settings instead of making the hard call. Every setting is a decision the designer was afraid to make.
- **"Users are asking for it"** — letting demand substitute for taste. Users asked for a better keyboard phone. Jobs gave them no keyboard at all.
- **"We can always remove it later"** — the feature ratchet. You almost never remove it later. Ship tight or ship bloated.
- **"It's already built"** — sunk cost keeping dead weight in the product. Jobs killed finished products (the 20th anniversary Mac, MobileMe features) when they didn't meet the bar.

### Examples

**Correct application — iPhone (2007):** Every phone manufacturer assumed a keyboard was mandatory. Jobs defined the experience: "You should feel like you're touching the content directly." A physical keyboard violates that feeling — your fingers are on plastic, not on the thing itself. Killed. One button. Touchscreen. The entire smartphone industry reorganized around this single taste judgment.

**Correct application — Apple product grid (1997):** Jobs returned to an Apple making dozens of products: printers, PDAs, monitors, multiple overlapping Mac lines. He drew a 2x2 grid: consumer/professional, desktop/portable. Four products. Everything else: killed. Apple went from near-bankruptcy to the most valuable company on Earth.

**Classic violation — Apple Lisa (1983):** Before Jobs learned radical curation, the Lisa team added features by committee. File manager with multiple views. Expandable memory. Office suite. Price: $9,995. The product did many things. None of them felt *right*. Commercial failure. The Macintosh team, under Jobs' tighter curation, shipped a year later and succeeded.

**Correct application — iPod scroll wheel (2001):** Dozens of interface proposals were rejected. Navigation by buttons, menu hierarchies, tiny joysticks. Jobs kept saying no until the scroll wheel emerged — one input that felt like flipping through a stack of records. The experience sentence governed everything: "1,000 songs in your pocket" required that accessing any of them feel effortless.

### Practitioners

**Steve Jobs (1955–2011)**
Co-founder of Apple, Pixar, and NeXT. Jobs' singular contribution was not invention — he invented almost nothing. It was taste applied as an engineering discipline. He treated aesthetic judgment not as decoration but as the primary filter through which every decision passed. Engineers and designers described working with Jobs as an exercise in hearing "no" until what remained was undeniable. The results — Macintosh, iPod, iPhone, iPad, Pixar's storytelling standards — all share the same signature: radical reduction governed by a clear experience vision.

**Dieter Rams (1932–present)**
Head of design at Braun from 1961 to 1995. His principle: *Weniger, aber besser* — "less, but better." Not less for efficiency. Less because what remains must be the best version of itself. Rams' ten principles of good design directly influenced Jony Ive and through him, every Apple product from 1997 onward. Jobs and Ive kept Rams' Braun products on display at Apple's design studio.

**Coco Chanel (1883–1971)**
Revolutionized fashion through curation and removal. "Before you leave the house, look in the mirror and take one thing off." Chanel's designs succeeded not by addition but by the taste to know what didn't belong. The little black dress: every unnecessary element removed until what remained was iconic.

### Historical Events

**Jobs Returns to Apple (1997)**
Apple was 90 days from bankruptcy. Jobs killed 70% of products in his first weeks. The company had printers, PDAs, a dozen Mac variants, the Newton. Jobs drew a 2x2 grid on a whiteboard and said: "This is what we're making. Everything else is cancelled." Within two years, Apple was profitable. The product that saved the company — the iMac — was itself a taste-gate product: one color (initially), no floppy drive (heresy at the time), USB only.

**iPhone Launch (2007)**
Jobs spent years defining what a phone should feel like. Cingular (AT&T) gave Apple unprecedented control: no carrier branding on the phone, no carrier bloatware, Apple controls the software entirely. This was the taste gate applied to the business deal — Jobs said no to every carrier that wouldn't accept his terms. The result: the first phone that felt like it was designed by one mind with one vision.

**Apple Lisa Failure (1983)**
The Lisa was Apple's first GUI computer. It was designed by committee with features added by consensus. The result was technically impressive and experientially incoherent. At $9,995, it sold 100,000 units. The Macintosh, developed under Jobs' tighter taste gate, launched a year later at $2,495 and became the template for personal computing.

**iPod Development (2001)**
Tony Fadell brought the hardware concept. Jon Rubinstein managed engineering. But the taste gate was Jobs'. He rejected interface after interface until the scroll wheel emerged. He insisted on "no more than three clicks to any song." He killed the file manager — you don't manage files, you play music. Every decision was filtered through one sentence: "1,000 songs in your pocket."

### Lineage

Zen Buddhist aesthetics of *wabi-sabi* and *ma* (negative space) (800 AD) → Bauhaus "less is more" (Walter Gropius, 1919) → Dieter Rams "less but better" at Braun (1961) → Steve Jobs at Apple (1976, refined 1997) → Jony Ive industrial design language (1997-2019)

Jobs explicitly studied Zen Buddhism under Kobun Chino Otogawa and credited it as foundational to his design philosophy. The connection is direct, not metaphorical: Zen's emphasis on removing the unnecessary to reveal the essential is the philosophical core of the taste gate.

### Origin

The taste gate as a design philosophy traces to Zen Buddhist aesthetics, where the removal of the unnecessary is itself the art. Jobs encountered this tradition through his Zen practice in the 1970s and 80s, studying under Kobun Chino Otogawa at the Los Altos Zen Center.

The Western lineage runs through the Bauhaus school (1919), which formalized "less is more" as an architectural and design principle, and Dieter Rams at Braun (1961-1995), whose "less but better" became the direct template for Apple's design language under Jony Ive.

Jobs' contribution was applying taste — which had been treated as a subjective, artistic quality — as a *hard engineering gate*. At Apple, taste was not a suggestion or a polish layer. It was the primary filter: products, features, and entire business units were killed because they didn't pass the taste test. This reframing — taste as engineering discipline, not aesthetic preference — is the specific innovation.
