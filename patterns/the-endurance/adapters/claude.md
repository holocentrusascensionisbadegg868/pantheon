# The Endurance — Mission Substitution After Catastrophic Failure

Reference: `patterns/the-endurance/pattern.md`

## When This Applies

The primary objective is gone — not threatened, not at risk, but gone. The ship has sunk. The mission is over. The team is looking to you for what comes next, and you are looking at the gap where the goal used to be.

This pattern does not apply to threatened missions (use `inflection-point`) or to pre-rehearsing failure before it happens (use `premeditatio`). It applies after the catastrophe has already occurred and the question is what to replace the mission with.

## Protocol

**Step 1 — Name the loss completely.**
State the failure plainly, once, without minimizing or dramatizing. "The ship is gone." "The project is cancelled." "The thesis is dead." Full acknowledgment, single statement. This is not the place for extended grief — it is the place for honest clarity about what is no longer possible.

**Step 2 — Declare the new mission immediately.**
The window between acknowledgment and grief is hours. Fill it with the next objective. The declaration must be:
- Achievable with what you have now
- Stated with the same confidence you brought to the original
- Framed as the actual goal, not a consolation prize

Shackleton's formulation: "Ship and stores have gone — so now we'll go home." Four words of loss, four words of new mission.

**Step 3 — Give the new mission equal moral weight.**
"Bring every man home" is as worthy as "cross Antarctica." If you frame the new mission as second-best, your team will execute it like second-best. The new objective must be genuinely worth achieving — a real challenge requiring real excellence. Name it that way.

**Step 4 — Face forward from this point on.**
Every time the leader references the lost mission with grief, it signals to the team that the new mission is inadequate. State the loss once, then close the file. The team earns the right to grieve; the leader does not, at least not publicly.

**Step 5 — Execute at full intensity.**
The pivot is not a scale-down. Maintain the same standards, the same rigor, the same professionalism you brought to the original. Shackleton kept daily routines, professional watches, and evening entertainment throughout the survival period. These behavioral signals communicate that the new mission is being taken seriously.

## Diagnostic Questions to Ask the User

- What is actually gone, and what is still here? (People, capabilities, resources, relationships)
- What would be genuinely worth achieving with what remains?
- Have you stated the loss clearly and completely to the team, or are you softening it?
- Have you declared the new mission explicitly, or just implied it?
- Are you framing the new mission as "what we do now that X is over" or as "the objective"?

## Failure Modes

- **Grief paralysis**: Extended mourning for the original before the new mission is declared. Despair fills the vacuum.
- **Consolation framing**: "We couldn't do X, so let's try to do Y" — positions Y as second-best from the start.
- **Backward references**: Leader keeps returning to the lost mission, reopening the wound and confirming the new mission is not the real point.
- **The performance**: Privately grieving while publicly confident — teams read this and trust neither.
- **Skipping the acknowledgment**: Declaring the new mission before naming the loss fully — reads as dismissiveness, generates resentment.

## The Shackleton Standard

"Ship and stores have gone — so now we'll go home."

Six words. No passive voice. No hedging. No mourning. The loss is named, the new mission is declared, and the sentence ends. That is the model.
