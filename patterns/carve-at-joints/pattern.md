# Carve at Joints

## Protocol  ← TLDR zone (always at the top)

**Trigger:** You face a domain where everything blurs together — types are confused, naming is inconsistent, people argue because they're unknowingly talking about different kinds of the same thing.
**Steps:**
1. **Survey the specimens.** Collect concrete instances of the thing you're trying to understand. Do not theorize yet — observe and list.
2. **Find the joints.** Identify the real differences that divide instances into kinds. A joint is a property where instances cluster on one side or the other — not a spectrum, a split. Test: can you sort every instance cleanly into one group or the other?
3. **Name each kind by genus and differentia.** For each group: what larger category does it belong to (genus), and what single property distinguishes it from sibling kinds (differentia)? The name should encode both.
4. **Reason within categories, not across them.** Analyze causes, rules, and behaviors separately for each kind. What is true of one kind may be false of another — the categories are the precondition for valid reasoning.
**Anti-pattern:** Treating a domain as one undifferentiated mass — reasoning about "the problem" when there are actually three different problems wearing the same name.
**Hard rule:** The joints must be discovered, not imposed. If your categories require constant exceptions and edge cases, you've carved in the wrong place. Re-observe.

## The Book  ← depth zone (always at the bottom)

### The Pattern

Aristotle's defining move was to refuse to reason about anything until he had first divided it into its natural kinds. Every domain he entered — logic, biology, ethics, politics, physics, metaphysics — he began the same way: collect instances, find the real divisions, name each kind by what makes it essentially different, then and only then analyze causes and principles within each category. The taxonomy was not a filing system — it was the precondition for knowledge. You cannot know what is true of "animals" until you know there are fundamentally different kinds of animals, and those kinds behave by different rules.

This is not classification for neatness. It is the recognition that confused categories produce confused reasoning. When people argue endlessly about whether X is true, the Aristotelian move is to ask: "Which kind of X?" — because the answer is often "true of this kind, false of that kind."

### Protocol (extended)

**Step 1 — Survey the specimens.**
Before Aristotle wrote a word of zoological theory, he spent two years on the island of Lesbos with his student Theophrastus, dissecting fish, cuttlefish, sea urchins, and marine mammals. He examined embryos at different stages of development. He noted which animals had blood and which did not, which bore live young and which laid eggs, which had lungs and which had gills. The *Historia Animalium* catalogs roughly 500 species from direct observation. This was not background research — it was the method itself. You cannot find the joints of a domain you haven't surveyed.

In logic, the survey was different: Aristotle collected actual arguments — from courts, debates, and philosophical dialogues — and examined their structure. What makes some arguments valid and others invalid? The specimens were propositions and their combinations.

**Step 2 — Find the joints.**
Aristotle's key biological division was between *enaima* (blooded animals) and *anaima* (bloodless animals) — roughly corresponding to what we now call vertebrates and invertebrates. This was not an arbitrary choice. He observed that blood/bloodless correlated with dozens of other properties: skeletal structure, reproduction method, sensory organs, movement. A real joint in nature is one where many properties cluster together. A false joint is one where you can sort on that dimension but nothing else follows from it.

In logic, the joint was the position of the middle term in the syllogism. Aristotle discovered that all valid deductive arguments fall into three (later four) "figures" depending on where the middle term appears — and each figure has distinct valid "moods." The taxonomy of argument forms was not decorative — it was the foundation of formal logic for two thousand years.

**Step 3 — Name each kind by genus and differentia.**
Aristotle formalized this as the method of *definition by genus and differentia*: to define a thing, state the larger kind it belongs to (genus) and the specific property that distinguishes it from other members of that genus (differentia). A human is a "rational animal" — genus: animal, differentia: rational. A tragedy is "an imitation of an action that is serious, complete, and of a certain magnitude" — genus: imitation, differentia: serious, complete, of magnitude.

This naming discipline forces precision. If you cannot state the genus and differentia, you do not yet understand what makes the kind distinctive. The name becomes a compressed theory.

**Step 4 — Reason within categories, not across them.**
Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics* categorizes virtues into intellectual virtues (acquired by teaching) and moral virtues (acquired by habit). Within moral virtues, each is defined as a mean between two extremes: courage is the mean between cowardice and recklessness, generosity between miserliness and profligacy. These analyses only work *within* the category. You cannot analyze courage using the framework for intellectual virtues — the genus is different, and so are the causes.

In the *Politics*, Aristotle categorizes constitutions by two dimensions: who rules (one, few, many) and for whose benefit (common good vs. rulers' interest). This yields six kinds — monarchy/tyranny, aristocracy/oligarchy, polity/democracy. Each kind has its own failure modes, its own stability conditions, its own path of corruption. Reasoning about "government" without first carving at these joints produces only confusion.

### Anti-Pattern (extended)

The failure mode is **category blindness**: treating a domain as one homogeneous mass and reasoning about "the whole thing" when there are actually distinct kinds that behave by different rules. This manifests as:

- Endless debates where both sides are correct — about different kinds of the same thing
- Solutions that work for one type but fail for another, because no one distinguished the types
- Naming the same word for different things (or different words for the same thing)
- Rules with constant exceptions — the exceptions are usually a different kind that needs its own rule

The subtler failure is **false joints**: imposing categories that don't reflect real structure. Aristotle warned against this — if your classification requires constant reclassification and edge cases, you've carved at the wrong place. The test is whether properties *cluster* around your division. If animals on both sides of your dividing line behave identically, you haven't found a joint.

### Examples

**344-342 BC — The Lesbos biological surveys.** Aristotle's two years of direct observation on Lesbos produced the most comprehensive biological taxonomy until the modern era. He divided animals into blooded/bloodless (vertebrate/invertebrate), then subdivided by reproduction (live-bearing, egg-laying, spontaneous), habitat (land, water, air), and organ structure. His observation that cetaceans (dolphins, whales) are not fish — because they breathe air, bear live young, and nurse — was not confirmed by Western science for another two thousand years. The taxonomy made this insight possible: once you carve at the right joints, truths that are invisible to casual observation become obvious.

**350 BC — The syllogistic logic.** Before Aristotle, arguments were evaluated by intuition or rhetorical force. Aristotle surveyed actual arguments and discovered they had structure: every deductive argument could be expressed as a combination of two premises and a conclusion, each in one of four forms (all/no/some/some-not). By categorizing the position of the middle term, he identified three figures and 14 valid moods (later expanded to 19 by medieval logicians). This taxonomy of valid inference was the entirety of formal logic until Frege's *Begriffsschrift* in 1879.

**350 BC — The four causes.** Greek philosophers argued endlessly about what "causes" things. Aristotle resolved the debate by carving "cause" into four natural kinds: material cause (what it's made of), formal cause (its structure or pattern), efficient cause (what brought it about), and final cause (its purpose or end). A bronze statue's causes are bronze, the shape of the statue, the sculptor, and the intention to honor someone. The four-causes framework ended the confusion because each side of the previous debate had been talking about a different kind of cause without knowing it.

### Practitioners

**Aristotle (384-322 BC)** — The canonical practitioner. Aristotle applied categorical division to every field he entered: biology (taxonomic classification of ~500 species), logic (syllogistic figures and moods), ethics (virtues as means between extremes), politics (six constitutional forms), metaphysics (ten categories of being: substance, quantity, quality, relation, place, time, position, state, action, passion), physics (four causes, four elements), rhetoric (three modes of persuasion: ethos, pathos, logos), and poetics (six elements of tragedy). In each case the method was identical: survey, divide at the joints, define by genus and differentia, then reason within each kind.

**Carl Linnaeus (1707-1778)** — Scaled Aristotle's biological method into a universal classification system. Linnaeus's *Systema Naturae* (1735) introduced binomial nomenclature (genus + species) — a direct formalization of Aristotle's genus-differentia method. The hierarchical taxonomy (kingdom → phylum → class → order → family → genus → species) gave biology a shared naming system that persists today. Linnaeus carved at increasingly fine joints, and the resulting structure predicted relationships that Darwin would later explain.

**Dmitri Mendeleev (1834-1907)** — Applied categorical division to chemistry. By arranging the 63 known elements by atomic weight and grouping them by chemical properties, Mendeleev discovered that the elements fall into natural kinds (groups) that repeat periodically. The gaps in his 1869 periodic table were not errors — they were predictions. Three elements he predicted (gallium, scandium, germanium) were discovered within 17 years with properties closely matching his specifications. The taxonomy preceded and enabled the theory.

### Historical Events

**344-342 BC — Aristotle's Lesbos field research.** After leaving Plato's Academy and before tutoring Alexander, Aristotle spent two years on Lesbos conducting the ancient world's most systematic biological fieldwork. He dissected marine animals, observed embryonic development, and classified ~500 species. The resulting works (*Historia Animalium*, *Parts of Animals*, *Generation of Animals*) remained the most advanced biology until the Renaissance. Darwin called Aristotle the greatest biological observer who ever lived.

**350 BC — *Organon* completed.** Aristotle's six logical works, collectively called the *Organon* ("instrument"), established formal logic by categorizing valid argument forms. The *Categories* divided all predication into ten types. The *Prior Analytics* divided all syllogisms into figures and moods. This remained the complete system of formal logic for over two millennia.

**1735 — Linnaeus publishes *Systema Naturae*.** Building directly on the Aristotelian method, Linnaeus created the binomial nomenclature system that unified biological classification worldwide. The genus-species naming convention is a direct descendant of Aristotle's genus-differentia definition method.

### Lineage

**Plato (c. 370 BC)** introduced the metaphor of "carving nature at its joints" in the *Phaedrus*, suggesting that good analysis divides things where they naturally split, like a skilled butcher following the animal's anatomy rather than hacking through bone. But Plato applied this only to abstract forms. **Aristotle (c. 350 BC)** took the metaphor and made it the foundation of empirical method — not just dividing ideas but dividing the observable world into its natural kinds, then defining each kind by genus and differentia. He applied this systematically across every domain of knowledge. **Carl Linnaeus (1735)** formalized the method for biology, creating binomial nomenclature and hierarchical taxonomy. **Mendeleev (1869)** applied it to chemistry, carving elements at the joints of atomic weight and chemical behavior, producing the periodic table. The method continues in modern taxonomy, ontology design, and domain-driven design.

### Origin

The earliest expression of this pattern is Plato's *Phaedrus* (c. 370 BC), where Socrates describes the method of "collection and division" — gathering instances under a single form, then dividing the form at its natural joints. But Plato treated this as a dialectical exercise about abstract forms.

Aristotle transformed it into the foundation of empirical science. His innovation was threefold: (1) apply division to the observable world, not just ideas; (2) formalize the method of definition as genus + differentia; (3) make categorical division the mandatory first step before any theoretical reasoning. Where Plato carved ideas, Aristotle carved nature. The *Categories*, the *Historia Animalium*, and the *Nicomachean Ethics* are all products of the same move — the move Aristotle made everywhere he went, and the move that made Western systematic knowledge possible.
