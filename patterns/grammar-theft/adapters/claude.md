# Grammar Theft

Reference the canonical pattern at `patterns/grammar-theft/pattern.md`.

## When to invoke

- User's work is producing better versions of the same thing but no structural novelty
- User is stuck inside a single framework and needs a breakthrough
- User is looking for the "next move" after exhausting a current approach
- User needs a creative direction and doesn't know where to find its structural grammar

## Protocol

**Step 1: Name the current system's limit.**
Ask: what problem does your current formal system generate that it cannot solve? This is the entry point. The limit defines what alien grammar to hunt.

**Step 2: Hunt for an alien system that solves the limit.**
Not aesthetically pleasing — structurally relevant. What domain, culture, or era has solved the specific problem you're stuck on? The domain should feel alien to your current work. The farther outside your domain, the less reverence you'll have for the source's limits.

**Step 3: Extract the structural logic.**
Do not import surface aesthetics. Ask: what *constraint* or *principle* is this alien system operating under? Express it as a rule that could be applied to anything. If you can't state it as a rule, you haven't extracted the structure — you've absorbed the style.

**Step 4: Apply as hard constraint to current work.**
Impose the rule on your current problem with full rigor. Ignore whether the result "looks like" the source. Push the constraint until it breaks, fails, or generates problems you couldn't have found within your original system.

**Step 5: Follow the failure.**
The problems generated at the constraint's limit are the entry point to the next stolen grammar. Treat exhaustion as a signal to hunt again, not a problem to fix.

## Anti-pattern to name explicitly

**Surface appropriation:** Taking the look of an alien system without extracting its structural principle. Result is pastiche — influence visible on the surface, original system's grammar unchanged underneath. The test: can you state the stolen principle as a rule that could be applied to any domain? If not, you borrowed aesthetics.

## Hard rule

The theft is not complete until the source is unrecognizable in the result. Visible resemblance to the source means you imported style, not structure.

## Usage notes

- This skill is most powerful when the user can name *both* the limit of their current system and a candidate alien system. If they can only name the limit, spend time on Step 2 hunting candidates.
- Remind the user: reverence for the source is the enemy. Picasso applied African mask logic harder than African mask-carvers ever needed to because his problem was different. Push past the source's own limits.
- The exhaustion signal (Step 5) is often misread as failure. Reframe: when a constraint generates problems it can't solve, those problems are the most valuable thing it produced.
