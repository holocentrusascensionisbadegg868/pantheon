# The Combination (Claude Adapter)

Reference the canonical pattern at `patterns/the-combination/pattern.md`.

## When This Applies

Invoke when:
- The user faces an industry where competition is destroying margins for all participants
- The user holds a structural cost or scale advantage that competitors cannot match
- The user is considering M&A, platform strategy, or ecosystem building
- The conversation involves "rationalization," "consolidation," or "how to deal with too many competitors"

Do NOT invoke when:
- Competition is healthy (margins stable, innovation continuing) — this pattern is for destructive competition, not normal competition
- The user lacks a dominant position — the combination requires being the natural acquirer, not a peer

## The Protocol

**Step 1 — Confirm the structural advantage.**
Before recommending the combination, verify: does the user actually hold a cost, scale, or infrastructure position that makes continued competition irrational for rivals? If not, this pattern doesn't apply — the user is a target, not an acquirer.

**Step 2 — Map the competitive landscape.**
Identify: How many competitors? Which are most exposed to the cost disadvantage? Which hold assets that would strengthen the combined entity? Who are the likely holdouts?

**Step 3 — Sequence the approach.**
Recommend starting with the most rational seller (most exposed, most likely to accept) and moving sequentially. Each acquisition strengthens the next offer. Do not approach multiple targets simultaneously — allow no time for coalition formation.

**Step 4 — Design the offer.**
Equity participation, not cash, aligns interests. Price at fair value at the moment of approach — not distress value. Show the math: what is the competitor's trajectory without combination vs. with it?

**Step 5 — Move within a single window.**
Define a time constraint. The combination that unfolds over years fails. Define the window and execute within it.

**Step 6 — Prove value through cost reduction.**
After combination, drive costs down visibly. The combination's durability depends on proving it creates value — not merely capturing it.

## Key Distinctions

**Combination vs. predatory pricing:** Predatory pricing destroys competitors; the combination absorbs them. The absorbed competitor's assets, relationships, and knowledge stay in the industry. The destroyed competitor's loss is a vacuum new entrants fill.

**Combination vs. cartel:** A cartel raises prices without improving costs — brittle and illegal. The combination drives costs down while stabilizing the industry — durable because it creates genuine economic value.

**Combination vs. vertical integration:** Vertical integration moves up and down the supply chain (own raw materials, distribution, retail). The combination moves horizontally across competitors in the same stage. Rockefeller used both; they are distinct moves.

## The Hard Rule

The combination must offer fair value at the moment of approach — before the target is broken. Once the offer becomes coercive (sell or we destroy you), the legal and moral exposure changes, resentful participants don't align their interests, and the durability of the combined entity suffers. Rockefeller's offer was: "here is the math showing why you cannot win; here is what we will give you to join us." That is a different conversation from "sell or die."
