# Methodical Doubt

## Protocol

**Trigger:** You are building on inherited assumptions, conventional wisdom, or a foundation you did not verify yourself — and the cost of a wrong foundation is high.
**Steps:**
1. **Suspend.** List every assumption the current approach rests on. Treat each as guilty until proven innocent.
2. **Doubt.** For each assumption, ask: "Can I construct a scenario where this is false?" If yes, set it aside — it is not bedrock.
3. **Find the floor.** Continue until you reach something that survives every doubt — the irreducible fact that remains true even in your worst-case scenario.
4. **Rebuild.** Starting only from what survived, reconstruct your understanding one verified step at a time. Each step must follow necessarily from the previous.
**Anti-pattern:** Building on sand — accepting inherited assumptions because they are familiar, then discovering the foundation was wrong after the structure is complete.
**Hard rule:** Never skip step 2 by declaring something "obviously true." Obviousness is the camouflage that bad assumptions wear.

## The Book

### The Pattern
Descartes's defining move was using doubt as a construction tool. Where others treated skepticism as destructive, Descartes discovered that systematic demolition of assumptions reveals what is actually load-bearing. The method is not to doubt for doubt's sake — it is to doubt everything so that what survives becomes an unshakeable foundation to build on.

### Protocol (extended)
1. **Suspend all assumptions.** Inventory every belief, convention, and "known fact" underlying the problem. Write them down. The act of listing makes the invisible visible — most assumptions operate below conscious awareness.
2. **Apply the doubt test.** For each assumption, construct a scenario (however extreme) where it fails. Descartes used the "evil demon" — a hypothetical adversary manipulating all perception. You need something analogous: what if this data is wrong? What if this market doesn't exist? What if this law of physics doesn't apply at this scale? If you can imagine it failing, it is not bedrock.
3. **Find the irreducible.** What remains after maximal doubt is your foundation. For Descartes, it was the act of thinking itself — you cannot doubt that you are doubting. In engineering, it might be a physical constraint or a mathematical identity. In business, it might be a customer behavior you have directly observed (not surveyed).
4. **Rebuild from the floor.** Chain deductions from your foundation upward. Each link must be as clear and distinct as the foundation itself. If a step requires an unverified leap, stop — you've found a gap that needs its own doubt cycle.

### Anti-Pattern (extended)
**Building on sand** manifests as: teams that inherit a system's assumptions along with its codebase; companies that accept market "truths" from analysts who accepted them from other analysts; engineers who trust abstractions they never verified. The failure mode is invisible until the foundation shifts — and then everything collapses at once because no layer was independently verified.

A subtler anti-pattern is **performative doubt** — going through the motions of questioning without actually being willing to discard an assumption. If you doubt everything but conclude that all your priors were correct, you have not applied the method — you have performed a ritual.

### Examples
**Analytic Geometry (1637).** Every mathematician before Descartes accepted that algebra and geometry were separate disciplines. Descartes refused to inherit this assumption. He reduced geometry to its simplest operations (distances, angles) and algebra to its simplest operations (equations, unknowns), and asked: what if these are the same thing expressed differently? The coordinate system was the rebuilt foundation — a single framework unifying both fields, enabling calculus a generation later.

**Optics and the Law of Refraction (1637).** In *Dioptrique*, Descartes refused to accept received theories of light. He stripped the problem to its simplest mechanical analogy (a ball striking a surface), derived the law of refraction from first principles, and redesigned lens theory from that foundation. Though his physical model was wrong, his mathematical result was correct — because the method forced him to separate what he could verify (angles, ratios) from what he was guessing (the nature of light).

**Challenger Disaster (1986, violated).** NASA engineers inherited the assumption that O-ring erosion observed in prior launches was within acceptable margins. No one applied systematic doubt to the safety model under the novel condition of low temperature. Richard Feynman's post-disaster investigation was essentially a Cartesian exercise: he refused to accept any safety claim he could not independently verify, and the ice-water O-ring demonstration was his "irreducible fact."

### Practitioners
**René Descartes** — philosopher, mathematician, scientist. Descartes spent years in self-imposed isolation, systematically demolishing everything he believed. The result was not nihilism but reconstruction: the *cogito* gave him a foundation, and from it he rebuilt epistemology, invented analytic geometry, contributed to optics, and established the framework for rationalist philosophy. His *Discourse on the Method* (1637) and *Meditations on First Philosophy* (1641) are the primary texts.

**David Hume** — applied the same radical doubt to causation and induction. Where Descartes found bedrock in thought, Hume found that even causation rests on habit rather than logic. This "Humean fork" between matters of fact and relations of ideas forced subsequent philosophy (particularly Kant) to rebuild on more honest foundations.

**Edmund Husserl** — developed the *epoché* (phenomenological reduction), a formal technique for suspending all assumptions about the external world to study the structures of consciousness directly. Husserl explicitly credited Descartes's method as his starting point, calling his own project "Cartesian Meditations."

### Historical Events
**1637 — Discourse on the Method.** Descartes publishes his four rules: (1) accept nothing as true that is not clearly known to be so, (2) divide difficulties into parts, (3) think in order from simplest to most complex, (4) make enumerations so complete that nothing is omitted. This is the first formal articulation of systematic doubt as a constructive method.

**1641 — Meditations on First Philosophy.** Descartes performs the full doubt sequence publicly: the senses deceive, dreams are indistinguishable from waking, an evil demon could fabricate all of mathematics. Only the act of doubting itself survives — *cogito ergo sum*. From this, he reconstructs the existence of God, the reliability of clear and distinct ideas, and the external world. The reconstruction is debatable; the method is permanent.

### Lineage
**Socrates (c. 400 BC)** pioneered systematic questioning — the elenchus — to expose contradictions in assumed knowledge. But Socrates was destructive; he tore down without rebuilding. **Pyrrho of Elis (c. 100 BC)** formalized skepticism as a philosophical stance, suspending judgment on all claims. Descartes inherited the skeptical tools but rejected the conclusion: doubt was not the endpoint but the starting gate. His 1637 method transformed skepticism from a philosophy of paralysis into a technology for building certainty. **Husserl (1913)** refined the method into phenomenological reduction. **Elon Musk (2002–present)** applied a simplified version — "first principles thinking" — to rocket economics, questioning every inherited cost assumption in aerospace manufacturing and rebuilding from physics and material costs.

### Origin
The method of systematic doubt traces to Socratic questioning in Athens, circa 400 BC, but Socrates never codified it as a constructive procedure. Descartes's innovation, articulated in *Discourse on the Method* (1637) and demonstrated in *Meditations* (1641), was to transform doubt from a conversational weapon into a solitary, systematic protocol with a defined terminus: the point at which doubt defeats itself. This made it repeatable, teachable, and applicable outside philosophy — to mathematics, physics, and eventually engineering. The specific trigger was Descartes's crisis of confidence during his years of travel (1619–1628), when he recognized that the scholastic education he had received was built on authorities who disagreed with each other. If the experts couldn't agree, their shared assumptions were suspect. The method was his answer: stop trusting authorities and rebuild from what you can verify alone.
