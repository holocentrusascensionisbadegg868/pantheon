# Schwerpunkt

## Protocol  ← TLDR zone (always at the top)

**Trigger:** You face a complex opposing system — a competitor, a problem, an organization, a technical architecture — with multiple nodes, and you must decide where to concentrate force to produce collapse, not just attrition.
**Steps:**
1. **Map the full system, not just the surface.** Identify every node: forces, dependencies, leaders, information flows, supply lines, belief structures. Do not plan attacks yet — observe the whole.
2. **Find the center of gravity.** Ask: "Which single node, if destroyed or captured, would cause the entire system to lose coherence?" This is almost never the largest or most visible node. It is the load-bearing one — the thing everything else depends on. In Alexander's campaigns it was consistently the enemy commander. In a technology company it may be the one person who holds all tacit knowledge. In a market it may be distribution.
3. **Design the secondary forces to hold, not win.** Everything that is not the decisive strike is an anvil — its job is to pin, fix, and slow the system while the hammer strikes. The phalanx didn't need to defeat the Persian infantry; it needed to hold them in place. Allocate minimally to secondary effort.
4. **Concentrate your best force at the decisive node.** The Companions were Alexander's fastest, hardest-hitting force — cavalry with the oblique wedge formation. The decisive strike must be your best weapon at the exact point of center of gravity. Partial force at the decisive node loses.
5. **Strike with speed that forecloses response.** Alexander's charges were not gradual advances — they were full-gallop assaults that closed the distance before the enemy commander could react. The gap between "we see the threat" and "the node is already gone" must be smaller than the enemy's OODA loop.
6. **Exploit the collapse, don't stop to consolidate.** When the center of gravity falls, the entire system loses coherence simultaneously. Pursue immediately into the disintegration — stopping to consolidate at the decisive node allows the system to reconstitute around a new center.
**Anti-pattern:** Uniform pressure — applying force evenly across the whole system, eroding every node a little, winning slowly through attrition. This is the failure mode of commanders and executives who don't identify the center of gravity and instead wear down the opposition on every front simultaneously. It is expensive, slow, and allows the opposition to adapt. Darius had more troops; Alexander had Schwerpunkt.
**Hard rule:** The center of gravity is never what looks like the hardest target or the most defended point. The most defended point is where the enemy expects you. The center of gravity is the load-bearing node whose fall makes defense elsewhere irrelevant.

## The Book  ← depth zone (always at the bottom)

### The Pattern

Alexander's defining cognitive move was structural analysis under pressure: before every major engagement, he surveyed the entire enemy formation and identified the single node whose collapse would dissolve the whole. Then he designed everything else — the phalanx, the light infantry, the allied cavalry — to hold the system in place while he personally led the Companions at that exact node with maximum speed and force.

This is not "attack the weakest point." The weakest point and the center of gravity are rarely the same. The weakest point is a flank that, if taken, forces a retreat. The center of gravity is the node that, if taken, makes retreat impossible because the army ceases to function as a coherent system. For three major Persian armies, that node was Darius himself — remove the king and 250,000 soldiers become a crowd. Alexander knew this and charged at the king while everyone else was fighting the battle.

The secondary elements are as important as the strike — they are the anvil that makes the hammer work. Alexander's phalanx was not trying to defeat the Persian infantry; it was trying to keep them engaged so they couldn't respond when Alexander broke through. Modern applications of this pattern always have this shape: a holding force that pins the secondary system, and a concentrated main effort that hits the decisive node.

### Protocol (extended)

**Step 1 — Map the full system.**
Alexander spent the hours before battle conducting personal reconnaissance. At Gaugamela he spent the night before riding the Persian lines to understand the formation. He was not looking for weak spots — he was identifying the structure of the system: who commanded, where the joints were, what would cascade if a specific node fell. Do not plan attacks until you understand the system topology.

**Step 2 — Find the center of gravity.**
Clausewitz's test: if this node is removed, does the system lose the will and ability to fight? The center of gravity is usually one of:
- The commander or leadership node (Alexander's most common target)
- The logistical hub (supply line, water source, port)
- The formation hinge (the joint between two forces that must cooperate)
- The belief/legitimacy node (the story that holds a coalition together)
Trace dependencies: what is everything else in this system dependent on?

**Step 3 — Design the secondary forces to hold.**
The holding force must be strong enough to prevent collapse and maintain engagement, but no stronger. Every unit assigned to the anvil is a unit not available for the hammer. Alexander was aggressive about minimal allocation to secondary effort — the phalanx held, it didn't need to advance. Calculate the minimum force required to pin each secondary system, and reassign the rest to the main effort.

**Step 4 — Concentrate your best force.**
The Companion cavalry was Alexander's fastest and most powerful striking arm. He led it personally. Whatever your best weapon is — fastest team, sharpest skill, most capital — it goes at the center of gravity. If your best force is occupied elsewhere when the decisive moment comes, you have failed before the engagement begins.

**Step 5 — Strike with foreclosing speed.**
The decisive strike must close the distance faster than the system can respond. At Gaugamela, when Alexander saw the gap forming between the Persian left and center, he didn't maneuver cautiously — he led a full-gallop charge through the gap and straight toward Darius. Speed is not just a tactical advantage; it is the mechanism by which the center of gravity is hit before the system can defend it. Slow decisive strikes become frontal assaults.

**Step 6 — Exploit the collapse.**
When the center of gravity falls, pursue immediately. Alexander's cavalry pursuit after Gaugamela turned a battlefield victory into the end of the Persian Empire — he drove all night while the Persian army disintegrated, preventing reconstitution. Stopping to celebrate or consolidate at the decisive node is the most common way to let a won battle become a drawn campaign.

### Anti-Pattern (extended)

**Uniform pressure** is the default behavior of commanders and executives who haven't identified the center of gravity. They feel responsible for every front, so they apply force everywhere proportionally. This produces attrition war — expensive, slow, unpredictable, and survivable by a determined opponent. It also describes most corporate strategy: attack on all fronts, build multiple products simultaneously, address every competitor equally. The opponent adapts.

**False centers of gravity** are targets that look decisive but aren't load-bearing. Killing the enemy general's staff is not the same as killing the general. Taking a competitor's best product is not the same as taking their distribution. The test is cascading coherence failure — does the system lose its ability to function, or does it route around the loss?

**Premature exploitation** — committing the main effort before the anvil has fixed the secondary systems — is the opposite error. If the holding force breaks before Alexander's charge reaches Darius, Alexander is surrounded. The timing requirement: the anvil must be locked before the hammer is released.

### Examples

**Gaugamela, 331 BC.** Darius built the largest army in history and leveled the battlefield to give his chariots room to operate. Alexander identified that none of this mattered if Darius fled. The entire operational design was: get the Companion cavalry to Darius before Darius could perceive the threat and respond. When the gap opened in the Persian line, Alexander drove through it at full gallop aimed at the Persian command standard. Darius fled. The largest army ever assembled dissolved in hours.

**Apple's Return from the Brink, 1997.** Steve Jobs returned to Apple as interim CEO, faced 90 days of cash left, and made one non-obvious move: he identified the center of gravity as Apple's product line complexity (350 products) and cut it to 10. Then he concentrated everything on one beautiful consumer desktop — the iMac. Not because the iMac would save Apple financially in the first quarter, but because it was the decisive node: restore the belief that Apple could build beautiful things, and partners, developers, customers, and employees would re-cohere around that belief. The system reconstituted from a single striking success.

**The Meuse Crossing, 1940.** Rommel's 7th Panzer Division reached the Meuse at Dinant on May 13, 1940. Standard doctrine required waiting for artillery and engineer support before crossing. Rommel identified the decisive node: the French command's model of the situation assumed Germans couldn't cross without preparation. The center of gravity was speed itself — not the far bank, but the collapse of the French decision loop. He crossed immediately with available forces, drove 50 miles before the French high command knew what had happened, and forced a strategic capitulation that no amount of prepared assault would have achieved.

### Practitioners

**Alexander the Great (356–323 BC):** Applied Schwerpunkt in every major engagement of his 13-year campaign from Greece to the Indus. His consistency was remarkable: regardless of terrain, enemy strength, or tactical context, he always designed around the same structure — phalanx as anvil, Companions as hammer, enemy commander as center of gravity. Undefeated in pitched battle.

**Carl von Clausewitz (1780–1831):** The Napoleonic Wars produced the theory Clausewitz codified in *On War* as *Schwerpunkt* — the point at which an enemy's strength converges, and from which all effort must flow. He had witnessed Napoleon apply the pattern at Austerlitz, Jena-Auerstedt, and Wagram. His formulation added the concept that the center of gravity is never fixed — it shifts as the campaign evolves, and the commander must continuously re-identify it.

**Erwin Rommel (1891–1944):** Applied Schwerpunkt as an operational doctrine in both World Wars. In 1940, he drove his Panzer division through the identified gap in French defenses at Dinant, then kept moving without pausing to secure flanks or wait for supporting units — maintaining speed as the weapon. His subordinates often didn't know where he was because he was always at the decisive point, not at headquarters.

**Steve Jobs (1955–2011):** In non-military domains, Schwerpunkt becomes: find the one product, capability, or narrative whose success would cascade through the entire system. Jobs at Apple in 1997 applied this exactly — cutting everything that wasn't load-bearing and concentrating on the one item that would restore systemic coherence. Each subsequent Apple product (iPod, iPhone) was another Schwerpunkt application: identify which node in the consumer electronics system is the center of gravity, then strike there.

### Historical Events

**Battle of Granicus (334 BC):** Alexander's first major engagement against Persia. Persian satraps positioned cavalry on the far bank to destroy the Macedonian cavalry as it crossed. Conventional advice from Parmenion was to wait and cross at dawn. Alexander charged directly across — not at the infantry, not at the weakest point, but at the satraps themselves. The center of gravity of Persian resistance in Asia Minor was the cohesion of the satrap leadership. Alexander killed three satraps personally in the first minutes. Resistance in the region collapsed within months.

**Battle of Austerlitz (1805):** Napoleon deliberately weakened his right flank and left the Pratzen Heights undermanned, baiting the Austro-Russian alliance into attacking his right. When the allied center thinned to reinforce this attack, Napoleon identified the heights as the decisive node — the point of ground from which both allied wings could be enfiladed — and drove Soult's corps at it with concentrated force. The heights fell in 40 minutes. Both allied wings, now separated and unable to support each other, were destroyed in detail. Considered the textbook application of Schwerpunkt in a European context.

**Rommel's Meuse Crossing (May 1940):** The French had a fixed mental model that German crossings required 2-3 days of artillery preparation. Rommel identified this mental model as the real center of gravity — not the river, not the French infantry, but the French command's decision cycle. By crossing before preparation was complete and driving 50 miles on the first day, he created a gap in the allied mental model that their command system could not close. The French surrendered in six weeks, not because they ran out of men, but because their command coherence collapsed.

### Lineage

The pattern runs from Alexander's campaigns (334–323 BC) through Hannibal's annihilation at Cannae (216 BC) — where the center of gravity was identified as the Roman formation's tendency to mass in the center — through Caesar's Gallic campaigns, to Napoleon's systematic exploitation of decisively breaking enemy command at Austerlitz, Jena, and Wagram.

Clausewitz codified the pattern as *Schwerpunkt* in *On War* (1832), written in the direct aftermath of observing Napoleon. The Prussian Army institutionalized it as operational doctrine, and the Wehrmacht applied it as *Auftragstaktik* (mission command built around identifying and striking the Schwerpunkt). The 1940 Fall of France is the doctrine's most decisive modern demonstration.

In non-military domains the pattern arrives through business strategy literature (Porter's "focus" as concentration of competitive effort, Christensen's "disruptive footholds"), through Jobs's product philosophy at Apple, and through modern venture-backed startup doctrine: don't compete across the board — identify the single leverage point in the market and strike there with everything.

### Origin

The pattern predates its theorization by two millennia. Alexander never wrote a treatise — the pattern is reconstructed from Arrian's *Anabasis Alexandri* (2nd century AD), written from primary Ptolemaic and Aristobulan sources. What Arrian makes clear across all accounts: Alexander's pre-battle reconnaissance was always focused on the same question — where does this army's coherence come from? His answer was consistently: from the man commanding it. Strike the man and the army becomes a mob.

Clausewitz generalized this into the concept of *Schwerpunkt* after watching Napoleon apply it repeatedly in the Napoleonic Wars. His formulation in *On War* (posthumously published 1832) is the first systematic treatment: "One must keep in mind that almost all strategy consists of the need to mass forces at one point, the Schwerpunkt — the point of main effort — and accept weakness everywhere else." The German military institutionalized this as doctrine and applied it in both World Wars.

The modern civilian version — concentrate all resources on the one product, decision, or move that is load-bearing and accept weakness everywhere else — runs through Jobs, Bezos (AWS as the decisive node in enterprise computing), and modern focused-fund venture investing. The structure is identical: map the system, find the load-bearing node, hold everything else with minimum viable force, and strike the node with maximum concentrated effort.
