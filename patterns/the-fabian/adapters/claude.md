The Fabian pattern applies when the adversary is stronger in direct engagement but needs a decisive victory to achieve their strategic goal. The weaker party wins by denying that victory — by surviving.

Reference the canonical pattern at `patterns/the-fabian/pattern.md` for full depth.

## Protocol

**Trigger:** The adversary is stronger in direct engagement AND they need to destroy your capacity to resist to achieve their strategic goal. You cannot win by fighting on their terms.

**Steps:**
1. **Name what the adversary actually needs** — not what they want (tactical victory) but what they need to declare strategic victory. "They need to destroy this [army / team / product / relationship]. If it survives, they have not won."
2. **Declare preservation the primary objective explicitly.** "The survival of [X] IS the campaign." Say it out loud. Without this, subordinates sacrifice the instrument to pride.
3. **Refuse engagements on the adversary's terms.** Retreat, delay, maneuver, avoid. Accept the appearance of weakness. Every day [X] exists is a day the adversary fails at their actual objective.
4. **Strike only when odds clearly favor you.** Washington attacked at Trenton. Fabius ambushed foraging parties. Select engagements for favorable odds, not optics.
5. **Track the adversary's attrition clock.** They are spending political will, money, or attention every day the conflict is unresolved. Know when that runs out.
6. **Build capability during preservation.** Valley Forge produced a better army. Use the survival period to train, consolidate, and improve.

**Anti-pattern:** Accepting battle to restore morale or prove you're not running. Never sacrifice the instrument to the feeling.

**Hard rule:** Never risk annihilation for a non-decisive victory.

## Application in Software / Organizational Contexts

- **Startup vs. incumbent**: Don't try to win the incumbent's market directly. Preserve runway, serve niches they can't reach, wait for their strategic attention to shift.
- **Technical debt battle**: Don't fight every legacy system at once. Preserve team velocity — identify the one system whose survival matters most and protect it while attriting the rest.
- **Organizational conflict**: When a more powerful stakeholder is pushing for a decision you can't openly oppose, don't fight frontally. Preserve optionality, delay, build coalition quietly, wait for their attention to move.
- **Underfunded project**: Don't spend resources proving capability through risky deliverables. Preserve core team, deliver small wins on favorable terms, track the budget clock on the competing initiative.

## The One Question

"What does the adversary actually need to win — and can I simply deny them that, by surviving?"
