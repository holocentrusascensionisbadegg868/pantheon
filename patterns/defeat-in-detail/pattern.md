# Defeat in Detail

## Protocol  ← TLDR zone (always at the top)

**Trigger:** You face multiple adversaries — enemies, competitors, opponents, or competing demands — that are currently separated and cannot fully coordinate against you. The threat is that they will unite before you can act.
**Steps:**
1. **Map the coalition's coordination timeline.** When can each enemy force arrive, unite, or communicate? The window you have to act is the gap between "now" and "when they converge." Name it explicitly.
2. **Take the central position.** Move between the separated forces so that you have interior lines — a shorter path to either opponent than they have to each other. The center is not the defensive position it appears; it is the position of maximum offensive advantage.
3. **Fix the secondary enemy with minimum force.** Detach the smallest force that can observe, delay, or threaten the secondary opponent enough to prevent them from moving freely. This is not a fighting force — it is a pinning force. Allocate minimally. Every unit assigned here is unavailable for the decisive strike.
4. **Strike the primary enemy with everything else.** Don't probe or skirmish — seek annihilation. A defeated-but-intact enemy reconstitutes. A destroyed enemy cannot threaten your flank when you turn.
5. **Complete the destruction before turning.** The pattern breaks if you leave the primary enemy functional. Napoleon's disasters were almost always cases where he broke off pursuit too early (Leipzig, 1813) or his detachments failed to hold the secondary enemy (Waterloo, 1815).
6. **Reverse and destroy the secondary enemy.** Now that the primary is eliminated, your pinning force becomes the anvil and your main force becomes the hammer against an opponent who has been waiting and watching while you grew stronger and they grew more anxious.
**Anti-pattern:** Fighting on multiple fronts simultaneously — dividing your force to "address all threats equally" — which converts your interior advantage into exterior weakness. This is the trap; defeat-in-detail is the escape.
**Hard rule:** The pinning force must be strong enough to hold but not so strong it weakens the decisive strike. If the pinning force fails and the secondary enemy moves freely, you face simultaneous enemies — the condition you were trying to avoid.

## The Book  ← depth zone (always at the bottom)

### The Pattern

Napoleon's defining cognitive move was recognizing that outnumbered forces are only outnumbered in aggregate. If the enemy coalition's separate armies cannot reach each other, you are not outnumbered — you are sequentially advantaged. His campaigns followed a consistent structure: assess when each enemy force could unite; position himself at the center between them before that moment arrived; pin one with a detachment; destroy the other with concentrated force; then reverse before the pinned force realized the main battle was over.

The key insight is geometric and temporal, not merely numerical. Interior lines — the central position — mean your forces travel shorter distances between threats than the separated enemies travel to support each other. This converts numerical disadvantage at the strategic level into numerical advantage at the point of each engagement. Napoleon never fought two enemies simultaneously if he could avoid it; he fought each enemy in sequence, always with local superiority.

### Protocol (extended)

**Step 1 — Map the coalition timeline.**
Napoleon's pre-campaign intelligence work was meticulous. He wanted to know not just where the enemy was, but *when* they could act. In 1796, the Austrian and Piedmontese armies were separated by the Ligurian Alps and slow courier communication — they could not reinforce each other in less than three days. That three-day window was the campaign. In 1805, the Russians were six weeks' march behind the Austrians. The campaign timeline was: destroy Austria in six weeks. He did it in five.

**Step 2 — Take the central position.**
The instinct of outnumbered commanders is to consolidate defensively. Napoleon's instinct was the opposite: move to the center *between* the enemies. At the opening of the 1815 campaign, he drove north and inserted the Armée du Nord between Wellington at Brussels and Blücher at Namur before either general knew where he was going. The center looks dangerous — you are surrounded on two sides. But it means you can reach either enemy before they can reach each other.

**Step 3 — Fix with minimum force.**
The detached force is the pattern's most demanding calculation. Too small and it fails to hold — the secondary enemy moves freely and you face simultaneous opponents. Too large and your decisive strike is weakened. Napoleon's rule of thumb was roughly one-third to one-quarter of his force for the fixing role, depending on terrain and the secondary enemy's aggressiveness. The Ney-Quatre Bras assignment in 1815 was correct in conception but failed in execution: Ney took Wellington's entire force as his objective rather than simply holding the road junction.

**Step 4 — Strike for annihilation.**
Napoleon explicitly rejected the 18th-century doctrine of maneuver for positional advantage. He sought the *destruction* of the enemy army as a fighting force. At Austerlitz, he didn't just beat the Austro-Russian force — he shattered it: 36,000 casualties and prisoners from an allied force of 85,000, the entire coalition dissolved the next day. A damaged army that retreats in order will reconstitute. Napoleon wanted surrender or pursuit-unto-destruction.

**Step 5 — Complete before turning.**
Leipzig (1813) is the negative example. Napoleon fought a three-day defensive battle against Austrian, Prussian, Russian, and Swedish forces — he had no interior position and faced the simultaneous combat the pattern is designed to prevent. The loss was not just military but structural: he never again had enough concentrated force to apply defeat-in-detail. The reversal phase (step 6) requires that the primary enemy is eliminated, not just retreating.

**Step 6 — Reverse against the pinned enemy.**
When the primary enemy collapses, the secondary enemy has typically been standing and watching, growing anxious, receiving no information about the main battle (Napoleon's cavalry screened aggressively). The reversal is often swift — the secondary force is demoralized, uncertain, and facing a force that just destroyed their ally. At Ligny in 1815, Napoleon destroyed Blücher's Prussians in an afternoon. Had Grouchy properly pursued, the reversal against Wellington would have had the same quality. Instead, Blücher reconstituted and returned.

### Anti-Pattern (extended)

**Simultaneous multi-front engagement** is the failure mode defeat-in-detail prevents — but it is also what defeat-in-detail can create if the pinning force fails. This is the pattern's structural vulnerability: the detachment is a calculated risk. If the secondary enemy defeats your fixing force and moves freely, you no longer have interior lines — you face coordinated enemies and your force is divided. The fixing force must hold.

**Premature exploitation** — turning before the primary enemy is destroyed — gives the primary enemy time to reconstitute while you are engaged elsewhere. Napoleon's pursuit cavalry was as important as his assault infantry; it was the mechanism by which "beaten" was converted to "destroyed."

**Waiting for perfect conditions** inverts the pattern. The entire logic of defeat-in-detail is that the window closes — the coalition will eventually concentrate. Napoleon moved when the window was open, not when his army was ideally prepared. The Italian Campaign of 1796 began with a starving, mutinous army. He moved anyway because the alternative was waiting for Austrian and Piedmontese forces to unite.

### Examples

**Italian Campaign, 1796.** Napoleon inherited command of the Army of Italy — starving, unpaid, equipment in disrepair, facing two separate enemy forces that together outnumbered him 75,000 to 37,000. His first action was not to attack either force but to drive through the mountain gap between them at Montenotte. Then: southwest to destroy the Piedmontese at Millesimo and Mondovì (four engagements in six days, forcing Sardinia to sue for peace). Then: reversed northeast against Austria, forcing Beaulieu back across the Po. In two weeks he had converted strategic disadvantage into sequential victories. He repeated this structure eight more times in the Italian campaign.

**Jena-Auerstedt, 1806.** Prussia declared war in October 1806, gambling that Napoleon couldn't respond before Russian forces arrived. Napoleon identified the window: Prussia's army of 130,000 was marching toward him through Saxony, while Russia's 90,000 were still weeks away. He drove north with 160,000 men, enveloped the Prussian army's flank before they realized the threat, and struck at Jena and Auerstedt simultaneously on October 14 — destroying both Prussian army groups in a single day. Russia arrived to find its ally had already capitulated.

**Waterloo Campaign as negative example, 1815.** The pattern's vulnerability: Napoleon correctly executed steps 1-2 (identified the coalition timeline, inserted himself between Wellington and Blücher at the opening). He partially executed step 4 (won at Ligny against Blücher). But Grouchy's detachment (step 3) failed to fix Blücher — the Prussian army regrouped and marched back to Wellington's position at Waterloo. Napoleon fought both enemies simultaneously on June 18. He was not defeated by Wellington alone; he was defeated by the simultaneous appearance of forces he had planned to fight sequentially.

### Practitioners

**Napoleon Bonaparte (1769–1821):** Applied defeat-in-detail in nearly every major campaign of his career. The Italian Campaign (1796-97) and the campaign of 1806 are the purest demonstrations. The pattern's failure at Waterloo (1815) is equally instructive — it shows exactly what happens when the fixing force fails and sequential advantages collapse into simultaneous combat.

**Stonewall Jackson (1824–1863):** Applied interior lines doctrine in the Shenandoah Valley Campaign (1862), keeping 17,000 Confederate troops mobile at the center of a Union cordon of 60,000 spread across three separate commands. He defeated each Union force in turn — Frémont at Cross Keys, Shields at Port Republic — before they could coordinate. Three Union armies failed to trap a force one-quarter their combined size because Jackson kept striking while they were separated.

**Antoine-Henri Jomini (1779–1869):** Napoleon's staff officer who codified the pattern in *Summary of the Art of War* (1838) as the principle of "interior lines" — the systematic advantage of the central position against a divided enemy. Jomini's formulation became the theoretical foundation for mid-19th-century military education in Europe and America.

### Historical Events

**Battle of Montenotte (1796):** Napoleon's first application as army commander. Austrian and Piedmontese forces separated by terrain. He attacked the junction of their lines — not the weakest point, not the largest force, but the gap between them — and divided them permanently. Within three days the two armies could no longer coordinate. He spent the rest of the campaign destroying them sequentially.

**Ulm Campaign (1805):** Austria mobilized and advanced into Bavaria before Russia arrived. Napoleon marched 200,000 men through the Black Forest in seven days — a movement his opponents considered physically impossible — and surrounded Mack's 60,000 Austrians at Ulm before the Russians were within 400 miles. Mack surrendered without a major engagement. Napoleon then turned east, met the Russian army at Austerlitz, and ended the campaign. He had defeated two separate armies using two separate decisive engagements before they could unite.

**Ligny (June 16, 1815):** First phase of the Waterloo campaign. Napoleon correctly identified Blücher and Wellington as separate and struck Blücher at Ligny while Ney held Wellington at Quatre Bras. Blücher was defeated. Had Grouchy pursued the Prussians south, the reversal against Wellington would have found an isolated British force. Instead Grouchy marched southeast while Blücher marched northwest — the fixing force went the wrong direction. Napoleon fought Waterloo two days later with Prussian forces arriving on his flank mid-battle.

### Lineage

The pattern was first articulated as doctrine by Jomini, who had watched Napoleon apply it across a decade of campaigns. Jomini's *Précis de l'Art de la Guerre* (1838) introduced "interior lines" as the central organizing principle of Napoleonic warfare — the mathematical advantage of the central position against exterior, divided forces.

American military schools absorbed Jomini in the 1830s-50s, and Civil War commanders applied interior lines on both sides. Stonewall Jackson's Valley Campaign (1862) is the Union-era American demonstration. Sherman's Georgia campaign (1864) applied the inverse — forcing Confederate forces to exterior lines by threatening multiple axes simultaneously.

Modern business strategy inherits the pattern through competitive theory: a startup with limited resources must not fight the dominant incumbent on every front simultaneously. The correct application is to find the market segment where the incumbent's forces are separated from their core, fix their attention with a limited engagement there, and build dominance in an adjacent segment before they can respond. Christensen's disruption theory is defeat-in-detail applied to product markets.

### Origin

The pattern predates Napoleon — Frederick the Great applied interior lines in the Seven Years' War (1756-63) against the Austrian-Russian-French coalition, fighting on multiple fronts by striking east and west alternately from Prussia's central position. Napoleon studied Frederick's campaigns as a young officer at the École Militaire.

What Napoleon added was operational speed. Frederick's interior line campaigns were slow enough that enemies could see the reversal coming and partially respond. Napoleon's corps system — multiple self-sufficient army corps that could disperse to forage and concentrate for battle within 24 hours — converted the geometric advantage of interior lines into a temporal weapon. By the time the secondary enemy understood the primary battle was lost, Napoleon's force was already reversing. The window between "primary enemy destroyed" and "secondary enemy reinformed" was the operational art Napoleon had mastered.

Jomini codified this in 1838, making it teachable. Clausewitz, writing simultaneously, was skeptical of Jomini's geometric formulation — he argued that war's friction meant interior lines rarely delivered their theoretical advantage cleanly. Waterloo proved Clausewitz right: the theory requires flawless execution of the fixing force, and friction in the Grouchy detachment undid the entire structure.
